Designing robust microbiome function.

We ask one central question: how do we design microbiomes whose metabolic function holds up in the real, fluctuating world? Our lab combines microbial ecology, data-driven modeling, and synthetic biology to engineer microbial communities that perform reliably — from agricultural soils to the human body.

Our Vision

From ecological understanding to engineered function

Microbes drive metabolic processes that shape food production, greenhouse-gas emissions, and human health. Yet microbial solutions — unlike chemical drugs — are living systems whose effects depend heavily on the surrounding nutrient landscape and the resident community. The MEFE Lab develops the design principles needed to make microbiome function robust: predictable and controllable across changing environments.

01

Understand

Identify the minimal ecological factors that govern community function under environmental change, using perturbation, time-series measurement, and theory.

02

Predict

Use machine learning on metagenomic data to find the interacting functional units that let us forecast and reason about community behavior.

03

Engineer

Apply synthetic biology to control those ecological levers and build consortia that deliver target metabolic functions reliably in the field and in the body.

Model Systems

Two systems, one set of design principles

We work across a complexity gradient so that the methods we develop generalize — and so our funding stays flexible.

Soil & rice-paddy microbiomes

The most complex system we study. We map how nutrient availability and the resident community govern greenhouse-gas cycling — with an emphasis on methane emission from rice paddies — to find the ecological levers for stable mitigation.

Nitrogen cycling Methane (CH₄) Metagenomics Consumer–resource models

The vaginal microbiome

A lower-complexity system with direct clinical impact. We design synthetic-biology approaches to treat recurrent bacterial vaginosis and, more broadly, to engineer microbiome solutions for inflammation.

Bacterial vaginosis Synthetic biology Nutrient-niche engineering Live therapeutics
