Research

Ecological engineering of microbiome function

Our central question is how to design robust microbiome function. We define ecological engineering as identifying the key variables that govern function in an ecosystem and tuning them — so that a target metabolic function holds steady across different environments and perturbations.

The Challenge

Why microbiome function breaks down

Microbial solutions are living systems. A consortium that boosts plant growth or clears a pathogen in a sterile lab often fails in the field or the body, because the real environment differs in two ways: the abiotic conditions (temperature, pH, nutrients) and the resident community it must compete with.

This is not a niche problem. Of the microbiome therapeutics approved by the FDA to prevent recurrent C. difficile infection, all remain fecal-transplant–based; every rationally designed defined consortium has so far failed to show efficacy in early trials. Designing microbiome metabolic function reliably is still at an early stage — and that is the gap our lab works to close.

Our Approach

Perturb · Measure · Model

A generalizable workflow for finding the few ecological factors that actually control function — demonstrated in soil nitrogen cycling (Lee et al., Nature 2025), where just two parameters predicted nitrate dynamics across all soils.

P

Perturb

Run controlled environmental-perturbation experiments on carefully assembled communities — varying pH, oxygen, electron acceptors, temperature, and carbon sources.

M

Measure

Track metabolite and gas dynamics with precise time-series measurement, paired with sequencing and metagenomics of the community.

M

Model

Use consumer–resource models and dimension reduction to collapse complexity into a few predictive parameters — the levers we can later engineer.

Research Directions

Three connected directions

Independent but mutually reinforcing efforts toward robust, designable microbiome function.

01

Microbiome response to environmental change

We identify the minimal ecological factors that govern function under perturbation. Building on our soil nitrogen-cycle work, we are extending the perturb–measure–model approach to agricultural greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O), with a focus on methane emission from rice paddies — modeling how the nutrient landscape and resident community (methanogens, methanotrophs) set emission.

Perturbation experiments Greenhouse gases Theory
02

Functional units via machine learning

Natural communities are high-dimensional. We use machine learning on sequencing and metagenomic data to find the right interacting functional units — the coarse-grained variables that predict community metabolic behavior and that map onto things we can manipulate.

Metagenomics Machine learning Dimension reduction
03

Engineering function with synthetic biology

We turn ecological understanding into control. Using synthetic biology — including nutrient-niche engineering and designed dependencies — we build consortia whose target metabolic function persists in realistic, competitive environments such as the host.

Synthetic ecology Niche engineering Live therapeutics
Model Systems

Where we apply these ideas

In the lab's first phase (~6–8 years) we build on two systems of contrasting complexity; later we expand to other environmental and host-associated inflammation systems through collaboration.

Soil & rice-paddy systems — most complex

High species diversity and strong spatial and physicochemical heterogeneity make soil the ideal testbed for finding minimal predictive variables. Our near-term focus is methane mitigation in rice paddies, building research resources rapidly through collaboration.

Vaginal microbiome — less complex

A tractable, clinically important community where we design synthetic-biology solutions for recurrent bacterial vaginosis — a model for engineering microbiome solutions to inflammation more broadly.
